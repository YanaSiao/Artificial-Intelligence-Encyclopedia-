The **Policy Gradient Theorem** and the **Performance Difference Lemma** are the theoretical pillars of modern reinforcement learning. While the theorem provides the "direction" for local improvements, the lemma provides a global "bridge" between the performance of any two policies.

### Performance Difference Lemma

The Performance Difference Lemma expresses the difference in expected return between two policies, $\pi$ and $\pi'$, in terms of the advantage function of one policy evaluated under the state distribution of the other. It is the fundamental tool used to prove the convergence of policy iteration and the safety of trust-region methods like TRPO and PPO.

**Definition and Meaning**

For any two policies $\pi$ and $\pi'$, the difference in their performance $J$ is given by:

$$J(\pi') - J(\pi) = \frac{1}{1-\gamma} \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^{\pi'}} \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi'(\cdot|s)} [A^\pi(s, a)]$$

- **$d^{\pi'}$**: The discounted state occupancy measure under the new policy $\pi'$.
    
- **$A^\pi(s, a)$**: The advantage of taking action $a$ in state $s$ according to the old policy $\pi$.
    
- **Intuition**: This tells us that if a new policy $\pi'$ consistently chooses actions that have a positive advantage relative to the old policy $\pi$ (weighted by how often $\pi'$ visits those states), then $\pi'$ is guaranteed to be better than $\pi$.


**Proof Sketch**

The proof relies on expressing the value function $V^\pi$ recursively and telescoping the sum over an infinite horizon.

1. Start with the identity $V^{\pi'}(s) - V^\pi(s)$.
    
2. Replace $V^\pi(s)$ with the Bellman expectation: $\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi'(\cdot|s)} [Q^\pi(s, a) - A^\pi(s, a)]$.
    
3. Expand the value difference as an expectation over the trajectories generated by $\pi'$.
    
4. Apply the definition of the discounted occupancy measure $d^{\pi'}$ to collapse the temporal sum into a single expectation over states.

### Policy Gradient Theorem

The Policy Gradient Theorem provides an expression for the gradient of the performance measure $\nabla_\theta J(\theta)$ that does not require taking the derivative of the state distribution $d^\pi(s)$, which is typically unknown and depends on the environment dynamics.

**The Theorem Statement**

For any differentiable policy $\pi_\theta$, the gradient of the performance objective is:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) \propto \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}} d^\pi(s) \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(a|s) Q^\pi(s, a)$$

This can be rewritten in a more practical expectation form for sampling:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^\pi, a \sim \pi_\theta} [\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a|s) Q^\pi(s, a)]$$

**The Proof (Sutton's Approach)**

The challenge in the proof is that changing $\theta$ changes both the actions taken and the distribution of states visited. The theorem proves these effects can be captured simply.

1. Differentiate the state-value function $V^\pi(s)$ using the product rule on $\sum_a \pi_\theta(a|s) Q^\pi(s, a)$.
    
2. The derivative $\nabla_\theta Q^\pi(s, a)$ involves the derivative of the next state's value $\nabla_\theta V^\pi(s')$.
    
3. Repeatedly unroll this recursive relationship (similar to the Bellman equation).
    
4. The unrolling process naturally produces the discounted state occupancy measure $d^\pi(s)$, effectively "summing up" the impact of the policy change across all future time steps.

### Usage and Application

**Bridge to [[Actor–Critic Policy Gradient | Actor-Critic]]**: The theorem justifies the use of a "Critic" to estimate $Q^\pi(s, a)$. As long as the critic is an unbiased estimator of the action-value function, the gradient direction is exact.

**Foundation for TRPO/PPO**: The Performance Difference Lemma is used to create a "surrogate" objective. Since we cannot easily compute $d^{\pi'}$ (the occupancy of the _new_ policy), we approximate it using $d^\pi$ (the occupancy of the _current_ policy). The lemma provides the theoretical bounds on the error introduced by this approximation, which leads to the "Clipped Objective" in PPO.

**Variance Reduction**: The theorem allows the substitution of $Q^\pi(s, a)$ with the Advantage Function $A^\pi(s, a)$. This is possible because $\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi} [\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a|s) B(s)] = 0$ for any baseline $B(s)$ that does not depend on the action.
